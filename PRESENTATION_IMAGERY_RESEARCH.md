# Presentation Imagery Research for Deck Director

## Core conclusion

In presentations, imagery is not decoration.
It is a decision system.

The practical formula is:

**Imagery effectiveness = narrative role × evidence value × emotional clarity × cognitive efficiency × style consistency**

If an image does not help the audience:
- understand faster,
- believe more,
- feel something clearer,
- remember the point longer,

then it is probably hurting the slide.

---

## 1. Every presentation image must have a job

Deck Director should classify imagery into one of these roles before generation:

### A. Atmosphere
Use imagery to establish tone, world, ambition, premium feel.

Best for:
- cover slides
- divider slides
- emotional transitions
- brand/world-building

### B. Evidence
Use imagery to prove a claim.

Best for:
- product proof
- screenshots
- before/after
- real-world field visuals
- venue/process/people proof

### C. Explanation
Use imagery to make an idea easier to understand.

Best for:
- process visuals
- frameworks
- annotated screenshots
- visual metaphors with real explanatory value

### D. Memory anchor
Use imagery to make the slide unforgettable.

Best for:
- turning-point slides
- quote slides
- big-stat slides
- closing moments

### E. Pace control
Use imagery to lighten the deck and create rhythm.

Best for:
- release slides after dense content
- section resets
- contrast moments in a long deck

---

## 2. Research principles that matter for imagery

### A. Picture superiority effect
Pictures are more likely to be remembered than words.

Implication:
- strong imagery is a memory multiplier
- weak generic imagery wastes one of the strongest recall tools in the deck

### B. Dual coding
People process verbal and nonverbal information through different channels.

Implication:
- pair a clear assertion with a useful image
- avoid text-only dumps
- avoid decorative imagery disconnected from the message

### C. Coherence
Remove irrelevant imagery.

Implication:
- no filler stock photography
- no random AI wallpaper
- no decorative clutter masquerading as meaning

### D. Signaling
Guide the eye to what matters in the image.

Implication:
- crop decisively
- isolate the subject
- emphasize only one key thing
- annotate sparingly when needed

### E. Spatial contiguity
Related words and visuals should stay near each other.

Implication:
- captions and callouts should not feel detached from the image
- reduce eye ping-pong

---

## 3. The imagery formula by slide type

### Cover slide
Formula:
- one dominant visual world
- one assertion headline
- minimal support line
- image must establish deck trust and tone immediately

### Divider slide
Formula:
- one atmosphere image or texture field
- one section title
- huge negative space
- image supports reset, not detail

### Narrative slide
Formula:
- one story image
- one point
- light support text
- image carries context/emotion/transformation

### Evidence slide
Formula:
- one proof image/object
- one claim
- one to three callouts max
- proof beats prettiness

### Framework slide
Formula:
- use imagery only if it clarifies
- otherwise use structure, icons, or diagrams
- do not force photography where clarity matters more

### Summary / close
Formula:
- one memorable image anchor
- three takeaways max
- image carries emotional residue into the close

---

## 4. Density rules for imagery

### One slide = one visual idea

Even if multiple image assets exist, they should feel like one system.

Avoid:
- unrelated photo collages
- multiple competing focal subjects
- too many visual stories at once
- decorative image overload

Deck Director rule:
- default to one dominant image or one tightly related visual unit

---

## 5. Credibility / trust formula

In presentations, image quality is not enough.
The image must also feel believable and appropriate.

**Credibility = relevance × specificity × professional finish × context fit**

Bad:
- generic smiling stock people
- random abstract AI visual that proves nothing
- flashy but fake-looking generated art

Good:
- specific to the industry/problem/world
- visually consistent with the deck
- supports the claim directly
- looks intentional, not generic

---

## 6. Crop survivability formula

Good presentation images survive slide composition.

Ideal traits:
- one dominant subject
- clear figure/ground separation
- negative space for headline if needed
- strong tonal separation
- can survive 16:9 crop
- can support thirds or 62/38 split

Bad traits:
- too many tiny details
- too many people/focal points
- weak contrast
- no obvious crop zone
- nowhere safe for text

Deck Director should score images for:
- subject clarity
- crop survivability
- text-overlay safety
- deck-style compatibility

---

## 7. Memory formula for imagery

Use one memorable visual isolate on important slides.

Examples:
- one dramatic human gesture
- one strong object silhouette
- one unusual crop
- one image with emotional charge

But do not overuse this.
If every slide tries to be the memorable one, the deck becomes visually numb.

---

## 8. Deck-level pacing formula for imagery

A strong deck varies image intensity.

Good cadence:
- dense strategic slide
- image-led release slide
- proof slide
- structure slide
- emotional image slide

Bad cadence:
- every slide full-bleed image
- every slide split-layout image/text
- every slide equally intense

Deck Director should use imagery as a pacing instrument across the sequence.

---

## 9. When not to use imagery

Do not force imagery when:
- the slide is purely structural
- a framework needs clarity more than mood
- the image would compete with the concept
- proof is better shown as chart/table/diagram
- imagery would reduce trust

Sometimes the right image decision is:
- no image
- subtle atmosphere only
- diagram instead
- screenshot instead

---

## 10. Recommended imagery system for Deck Director

### Tier 1 — must-have
1. every image has a role
2. one slide = one visual idea
3. specificity over generic stock
4. credibility over spectacle
5. image must survive crop and text placement

### Tier 2 — quality multipliers
6. assertion + image/evidence pairing
7. signaling inside the image
8. one memorable isolate on key slides
9. vary image intensity across the deck

### Tier 3 — system behavior
10. image role should be archetype-aware
11. image dominance should match slide function
12. image style should remain consistent with deck DNA
13. imagery should contribute to pacing across a long deck

---

## 11. Implementation direction for Deck Director

Before generating a slide image, the system should decide:

1. does this slide need imagery at all?
2. what is the image’s role?
3. what image mode is best?
   - documentary photo
   - cinematic concept
   - screenshot
   - annotated proof
   - atmosphere texture
   - no image
4. how dominant should the image be?
   - full bleed
   - split layout
   - inset
   - background support
   - no image
5. what should the audience understand in two seconds?
6. what should the audience remember afterward?

That turns imagery from prompt decoration into presentation intelligence.
